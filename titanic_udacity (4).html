Could you have survived the Titanic?!!¶

We all know about the luxurious streamline - RMS Titanic which killed around 1500 passengers during its maiden trip to New York! After a lot of research, its believed that once lauded as state-of-art design, had many flaws. In other words, unfortunately, it was bound to be doomed!

With the dataset from Kaggle, I want to explore the chances of surviving this disaster!

Some of my research questions are: How many passengers survived? Did the class of passenger matter? If yes, What proportion of lower class passengers survived the disaster? What was the proportion of females and males travelling? Did women and children have more chances of survival than the others?

import re
def getCoachNames(onboarder):
    """This function extracts the deck names from the cabin column"""
    coaches = onboarder
    regex = re.compile('[^a-zA-Z]')
    return regex.sub('', coaches[0])

titanic['CabinNames'] = titanic[['Cabin']].apply(getCoachNames, axis = 1)

def isChild(onboarder):
    """This function uses the condition 'Age < 21' to divide the data further into Male/Female/Child"""
    Age, sex = onboarder
    if Age < 21:
        return 'child'
    else:
        return sex

titanic['status'] = titanic[['Age','Sex']].apply(isChild, axis = 1)

Conclusion¶

After playing around with the data, we come to see that the minimum age of the passengers was around 4 months and maximum age was 80 years old! These passengers were either travelling alone or together with atmost 6 family members.The given dataset had a lot of missing values, especially in columns Age, Cabin and Embarked. Since the column 'Cabin' will not tell us much about the survivability of the passengers, replacing those missing values wouldn't add much weight to the analysis. Thereby, I have decided to replace the missing values in column 'Age' by median and 'Embarked' by the most frequent item.

The dataset contains 890 rows of passenger details. Since we know that there were children on-board too, to find the distribution amongst these three categories i.e., Male, Female and Child, I have decided to use the 'age' variable for this. Given the fact that anyone above the age of 21 is an Adult. Using this condition, I have bifurcated the dataset into three groups. After doing so, there were 53% males, 26.5% - females and 20% - children. By looking at the plots of - 'Distribution of Passenger Demographics' we can draw conclusion regarding the survivability of each type. Males had a survival rate of 17% whereas females had 76% and children 45% of survival rate.

Now exploring the data by classes, we had three types of classes namely - First class a.k.a the luxury class, Second class and Third class. Looking at the survival rate of each class, the ones travelling in third class had a very bleak chance compared to the ones in First class. The upper class had a survival rate of 64%, middle class had around 47% and the lower class around 24%, which means that the person travelling in first class had almost three times more chance of survival compared to their third-class counterparts. Unfortunetly, third class had the most number of men and children travelling!

The Distribution by Age histogram shows that majority of the passengers are between the age group 20-30. Again from the barplot with Age and Gender, it shows that younger children had higher chances of survival. Interestingly, the port of origin shows some relation with the rate of survival. The ones who embarked the ship at Cherbourg have survival rate of 55% where as the ones who boarded at Queenstown have 39% & at Southampton have 34%. One more noticeable fact is that more than 500 passengers or so where travelling solo. Though the ones travelling with family had a survival rate of 50% whereas the ones going alone had only 30%. Maybe this was one of the reason why there were so many casualties!

Finally, to check if my exploratory analysis about the variables that could help us predict the rate of survival I decided to find the correlation matrix between the variables comparing it to the boolean variable- Survived. From the matrix, most of the variables like Age, Class etc have negative correlation with our dependent variable. On the other hand, Fare and Parch have some correlation with it. This negative correlation for these variables could be because of some biases in the way this sample was collected. Originally the total number of people on-board were around 1357 but the data here is only 890, since we don't really know how this data was sampled, therefore the rate of survivability will not be a true representative of the population.

In conclusion, I feel the variable list provided here isn't enough to draw a definite insight on the survivability of the passengers on Titanic. One important variable which I think should be present is - 'Crew Member or Not'. Since with this information we can differentiate between the true travellers and crew on-board. One of the main reason that led to such a huge loss of lives is because there weren't enough lifeboats. Linking this reason to the socio-economic factors back then, I think variables like physical demographics - 'Race' and 'Occupation' will help us understand the situation since probably there was some sort of discrimination on who gets onto the life-boats first etc.
